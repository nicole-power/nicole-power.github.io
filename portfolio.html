The Dangers of Performative Activism

The common rhetoric oft heard reverberating through the likes of Baby Boomers of “Millennials are killing XYZ Industry” is a multi-faceted issue. Where there are companies and industries Millennials decide to support with their money there will of course always be others that fall to the wayside of obsoletion. The reality of the situation, of an economy that does not allow for the luxuries enjoyed by older generations, has seen a rise in businesses striving to appeal to the Millennial mindset; to the demand for more sustainable business models and eco-friendly practices that put at ease the anxiety of contributing to the mass pollution of an environment the younger generations will inherit.

But what of companies that choose to exploit these concerns, that would offer a symbolic victory that placates consumers but in reality offers negligible improvements towards the continued pollution of the environment. Take the airline industry for example, where in past years airlines have united in ditching plastic straws on board their flights while refusing to acknowledge the amount of emissions they produce in their travels throughout the world. The appearance of sustainability as opposed to the reality of striving to build better business practices for our environment are often, in my experience, little more than performative activism in the name of capitalism.

I have had the opportunity to work for one of the largest book retailers in Canada and found that the business took great pains to present an eco-friendly face to their consumers in order to appeal and appease a desire for sustainability. Where do the failings of performative activism display themselves? Away from the eye of the consumer, where they may purchase their reusable water bottles and literature on how to reduce one’s carbon footprint, in a bag made of paper not plastic, unaware that mere feet from them in the backroom these items arrive every morning more often than not in individually wrapped plastic packaging.

I have all the respect in the world for the individual that strives to lessen their environmental impact but buying into and giving one’s money to a corporation whose desire to appear sustainable is little more than a money grab is a misguided approach. This is especially evident when the eco-friendly appearance does not permeate every layer of their production line, when the arrival and unpackaging of new products is associated with bags upon bags of non-biodegradable plastic.

The United Nations projects that airplane emissions of carbon dioxide will have reached over 900 million metric tons in 2018, and then triple by 2050. So while the refusal to use plastic straws and switching from plastic to paper bags is of course a step in the right direction, it’s barely even a baby step; it’s an infinitesimal move that does little to remedy the airline industry’s impact on the environment and rather serves to act as a victory for environmentalism that is solely symbolic in nature.

Acts such as these fool individuals into believing that the companies they support are doing their part to be more sustainable and does not allow for a conversation that digs at the deeper issues still plaguing our environment. It would appear evident that these companies are motivated not by an interest in the health of our planet but rather in the interest of lining one’s pockets with the money of Millennials and Gen Z. It is an act that fundamentally lacks empathy, to exist in the laps of luxury during one’s brief stay on Earth while actively disregarding future generations; those who will inherit the environment that has already begun to show its damage and will continue to do so until younger generations demand proper action. It’s time we kill the industries that indulge in performative activism.

Movie Review Sunday

By Nicole Power | Entertainment | May 12, 2018 at 3:00pm

How to Talk to Girls at Parties is a science fiction film that is based on a short story by Neil Gaiman. The film is set in 1970’s London and follows a teenaged boy, Enn, who is enmeshed in the local punk rock scene. Enn encounters a small collection of alien colonies where he meets Zan, an interstellar visitor who takes the form of a teenage girl. Though the two characters come from vastly different backgrounds they both exude the restlessness that emerges in the rebelliousness of youth. And arguably what is more emblematic of teen angst and rebelliousness than punk rock? All in all the film was an enjoyable watch regarding young love blossoming in the wake of alien visitation. If you, like me, enjoy relatively loony romantic comedies then this is the movie for you!

8/10

Tangles; A Harrowing Tale of Love and Loss to Alzheimer's

By Nicole Power | Literature | May 12, 2019 at 12:00pm

In Sarah Leavitt’s graphic memoir Tangles the author constructs a narrative surrounding the loss of her mother to Alzheimer’s and the associative ramifications this causes on herself as well as her family leading from diagnosis to the illness’s progression. I found that the graphic novel format added a level of rawness to an already deeply personal story; the illustrations are beautiful and alluring and though they lack the polish of a professional this does not hinder the narrative but rather adds a layer of intimacy to the story. I think it’s important to make experiences about mental illnesses and diseases more accessible so that we as a society may be made more knowledgeable. This harrowing tale of the slow degeneration involved in losing one’s mother to Alzheimer’s sheds light on what we still do not know about the disease and the ways in which we may approach the subject of mental illness with much more care and dignity in our day-to-day lives.

The Duality of Oppression Experienced by Women of Colour in Nella Larsen’s Passing

By Nicole Power | Literature | February 21, 2019 at 3:00pm

Nella Larsen’s Passing explores the themes of oppression experienced not only by people of color but those facing the multi-layered subjugation from society of being born a black woman. In a country that was built and prospered on the exploitation of slavery Larsen’s novel discusses the implications of the white populations continued reliance on the skewed power imbalance amongst races; this is achieved through the perpetuation of baseless hatred foisted onto the black community through white eyes simply for existing in the human form of a darker shade. Passing is a virulent critique against the white oppressors that Larsen viewed in her society and yet there are instances in which she can be interpreted to rally against their power in her novel. I highly recommend this work, in particular to those looking to educate themselves on the pervasiveness of systemic racism and microaggressions that often run unchecked in society today.
